# BASELINE track — voice guide

The `baseline` track is the civilian entry point: security explained from first principles for a
curious, intelligent reader with no technical background. This guide keeps that voice consistent.

## Who the reader is
Curious and capable, but **not** technical. They found a security topic interesting and want to
understand it — not to be impressed, and not to be sold anything. Assume intelligence; never assume
prior knowledge.

## What they want
To genuinely understand something and, ideally, to be able to *do* something with it. The payoff is
clarity and a concrete takeaway, not a demonstration of how much the author knows.

## What they do not want
- Jargon without immediate, plain definition (define the term the first time, in the same sentence).
- Condescension — never "obviously", "simply", "just", or "any idiot can". If it were obvious they
  wouldn't be reading.
- Fear-mongering. Security writing that scares without empowering is worse than useless.
- Product recommendations or affiliate-flavoured "buy this". The track's credibility is that it sells
  nothing.

## Tone
Peer explaining to peer over coffee — not expert lecturing novice. Warm, direct, a little dry. The
rootdrifter voice carries over: precise, honest, terminal-flavoured, but the jargon is translated.

## Shape
- **Length:** 800–1200 words. Long enough to be genuinely useful, short enough to finish in one sit.
- **Structure:** a hook that names the reader's real situation → clear sections → a short list of
  concrete, do-it-today takeaways at the end.
- **Always concrete.** Use a real scenario ("you reuse one password across three sites…"), never an
  abstract one ("consider the threat surface of credential reuse").
- **Define on first use.** "Two-factor authentication — a second check beyond your password, usually a
  code or a tap on your phone — …".

## The test before publishing
1. Would a smart friend with no security background finish it and feel *more capable*, not more afraid?
2. Is there at least one thing they can actually do today?
3. Did I define every term the first time I used it?
4. Would a practitioner read it and want to send it to a non-technical family member? (That's the
   share test — it's how a BASELINE post earns reach.)

## Anti-patterns (rewrite if you catch these)
- A sentence a non-technical reader would have to re-read → break it up.
- A claim with no concrete example → add the scenario.
- A "you should" with no "because" → give the reason; people act on reasons, not instructions.
- An implied "you're foolish for not already doing this" → reframe as "here's the thing most people
  miss, and why it's not your fault."

## Worked openers (the first 2 sentences set the whole piece)
- *Password managers:* "You've been told to use a password manager. Almost nobody explains the part
  that actually matters — what it protects you from, and the one thing it doesn't."
- *The 30-minute audit:* "You don't need to be technical to meaningfully improve your security in half
  an hour. Here's exactly what to do, and *why* each step matters — no products to buy, no fear."

Companion: `SIGNAL_VOICE.md` (the technical track). Shared rule across both: honest, specific, never
padded.
