<blockquote>Practitioner notes, grounded in a causal-inference research project over a large public phishing-email corpus and an in-progress Wazuh home SIEM lab. No vendor affiliation; no production telemetry reproduced here.</blockquote><p>Most detection logic is correlational, and most of the time that is fine. A rule fires because some feature — a sender domain, a byte pattern, a process-launch sequence — co-occurred with badness in the data it was built from. The problem is that a correlation is a description of the past, and the thing you are detecting has a budget and a motive to make the future look different. Correlational detection learns what attacks looked like last year. Under adversarial pressure that is precisely the wrong thing to learn.</p><h2 id="why-correlation-breaks-under-an-adversary">Why correlation breaks under an adversary</h2><p>A spam filter that learned "emails mentioning a particular payment processor are 80% phishing" has not learned anything about phishing. It has learned a property of last year's <em>campaigns</em>. The mechanism that makes a phishing email work — manufactured urgency, an authority cue, a credential-harvesting destination — is causal and durable. The surface features a correlational model latches onto — specific domains, specific phrasings, specific headers — are incidental and cheap to change. So the attacker does the rational thing: they hold the mechanism constant and perturb the surface. The filter's accuracy on last quarter's data was real; its accuracy on next week's campaign is a coin toss, because the features it relied on were never the cause.</p><p>This is the detection-engineering version of a well-known statistical trap: a model can have excellent fit and zero robustness if it learned confounders instead of causes. In a benign domain that is a quality problem. In security it is an exploitable one — the gap between "fits the training data" and "captures the mechanism" is the attacker's entire operating room.</p><h2 id="what-causal-actually-means-here">What "causal" actually means here</h2><p>The shift is from <em>"what co-occurs with attacks?"</em> to <em>"what, if I intervened on it, would change whether this is an attack?"</em> That second question is the language of causal inference, and it has formal machinery behind it. You express your assumptions as a directed acyclic graph (a DAG) — nodes for the variables, edges for the claimed causal relationships — and that graph tells you which associations are genuine causal paths and which are confounded backdoors you should be ignoring.</p><p>The research project this draws from did exactly that over a large phishing corpus: construct a DAG of the hypothesised mechanism (intent → tactic → observable artefact), then use causal tooling — the <code>DoWhy</code> framework — to estimate effects along the real paths and, crucially, to <strong>refute</strong> them with placebo and random-common-cause tests. A correlation that evaporates when you add a placebo treatment was never a mechanism. That refutation step is the part most detection work skips, and it is the part that tells you whether you have found a cause or a coincidence.</p><h2 id="an-honest-caveat-about-the-llm-shortcut">An honest caveat about the LLM shortcut</h2><p>Constructing these DAGs by hand does not scale, and one tempting accelerant is to have a large language model propose the causal graph from the data and the domain description. It works — to a point — and the point is worth stating plainly: model-proposed graphs varied substantially in how well they aligned to a human-validated ground truth. A capable model produced graphs that mostly agreed with expert structure; weaker models produced confident graphs that did not. The lesson for a SOC is not "don't use the model." It is "the model is a hypothesis generator, not an oracle" — every machine-proposed causal claim still has to survive the same refutation tests you would apply to your own. Adversarial robustness does not come from a smarter autocomplete; it comes from the discipline of testing the mechanism.</p><h2 id="from-research-to-the-soc-floor">From research to the SOC floor</h2><p>You are not going to run a causal-inference pipeline on every alert, and you should not try. The contribution is to <em>detection design</em>, and it shows up in three concrete habits:</p><ul><li><strong>Detect on the mechanism, not the indicator.</strong> The Pyramid of Pain already says this in ATT&CK terms — hashes and IPs are trivial for an attacker to change, TTPs are expensive. Causal thinking is the formal version: write the detection against the step the attacker <em>cannot</em> skip (the token-impersonation call, the credential read, the C2 beacon's behavioural shape), not the artefact they can swap.</li><li><strong>Interrogate your features for confounders.</strong> Before trusting a high-performing rule, ask what it is actually keyed on. If a "malware" detection is really detecting "this one packer," you have learned a campaign, not a behaviour — and you will miss the next campaign with total confidence.</li><li><strong>Build the refutation step into validation.</strong> This is where the home lab earns its place. Every detection scenario I build gets exercised against its own technique and against benign baseline traffic — the lab's version of a placebo test. A rule that also fires on the benign baseline has failed its refutation, exactly as a causal estimate that survives a placebo has passed one.</li></ul><h2 id="the-takeaway">The takeaway</h2><p>Correlational detection is not wrong; it is brittle in exactly the dimension that matters when something is trying to evade you. Causal reasoning will not give you a magic rule, and the tooling will not save you from bad assumptions. What it gives you is a question to hold every detection up against — <em>would this survive an attacker who changed everything except the thing that makes the attack work?</em> — and a method, refutation testing, to answer it honestly. That question is cheap to ask and it is the difference between a detection that ages well and one that was already obsolete the day the campaign rotated.</p>
