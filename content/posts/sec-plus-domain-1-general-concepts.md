<p><em>Study notes reframed for a blog audience — the foundational vocabulary of CompTIA</em><br><em>Security+ (SY0-701) Domain 1, and why each idea earns its place.</em></p><p>Domain 1, <strong>General Security Concepts</strong>, is 12% of the exam and the grammar of everything that<br>follows. Get these straight and the rest of the syllabus stops feeling like a list of acronyms.</p><h2 id="control-categories-and-types">Control categories and types</h2><p>Controls are sorted two ways at once: by <strong>category</strong> (technical, managerial, operational,<br>physical) and by <strong>type</strong> (preventive, detective, corrective, deterrent, compensating,<br>directive). A CCTV camera that is <em>visible</em> is a deterrent; the footage it records is detective.<br>The exam tests that you can hold both axes in your head for the same control.</p><h2 id="the-cia-triad-and-non-repudiation">The CIA triad and non-repudiation</h2><p>Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — and the often-forgotten fourth idea, <strong>non-repudiation</strong><br>(you cannot deny having done something). A digital signature delivers integrity <em>and</em><br>non-repudiation because only your private key could have produced it. Hashing alone gives integrity<br>but not identity — a distinction the exam probes directly.</p><h2 id="zero-trust">Zero Trust</h2><p>"Never trust, always verify." The high-yield detail is the vocabulary of the planes: the <strong>Policy</strong><br><strong>Decision Point</strong> decides, the <strong>Policy Enforcement Point</strong> enforces. Real systems implement this as<br>per-request authorisation rather than a one-time perimeter check — which is exactly why<br>compartmentalised architectures (a phone split into independent encrypted profiles, a workstation<br>that filters its own DNS before egress) feel natural once Zero Trust clicks.</p><h2 id="pki-and-certificates">PKI and certificates</h2><p>The deepest sub-topic. Know the trust chain (root → intermediate → leaf), and know the two<br>revocation mechanisms — <strong>CRL</strong> (a pulled list) versus <strong>OCSP</strong> (a real-time query). Certificate<br>validity and revocation is a classic exam trap because the "expired vs revoked vs untrusted-CA"<br>distinctions look similar under time pressure.</p><h2 id="the-portfolio-connection">The portfolio connection</h2><p>These concepts are not abstract once you have built something with them. A FIDO2 hardware key<br>enrolled to unlock a disk <em>is</em> "something you have" plus physical presence; a verified-boot key<br>hash you can check against a vendor's published value <em>is</em> a root-of-trust attestation. The<br>vocabulary is easier to keep when it names things you have actually wired up.</p><h2 id="exam-traps-to-watch">Exam traps to watch</h2><ul><li>Mixing the control category axis with the control type axis.</li><li>Assuming hashing provides non-repudiation (it does not).</li><li>Inverting CRL and OCSP.</li></ul><p><em>Full domain notes, scenario drills, and a quick-reference card are in the</em><br><em><a href="https://github.com/rootdrifter/sec-plus-notes">sec-plus-notes</a> repo.</em></p>
