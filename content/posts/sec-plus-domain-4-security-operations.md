<p><em>Study notes reframed for a blog audience — what Domain 4 of CompTIA Security+ (SY0-701)</em><br><em>actually means on a SOC shift, not just what to memorise for the exam.</em></p><p>Domain 4, <strong>Security Operations</strong>, is the largest slice of the SY0-701 exam at <strong>28%</strong> — and it<br>is no accident that it is also the day-to-day reality of a SOC analyst. If you want to know what<br>the job <em>is</em>, read this domain.</p><h2 id="why-this-domain-is-the-soc">Why this domain is the SOC</h2><p>Everything else in the exam describes a control or a concept. Domain 4 describes the <strong>loop you</strong><br><strong>live in</strong>: monitor, detect, triage, investigate, respond, recover, improve. The exam tests whether<br>you can apply that loop under realistic pressure.</p><h2 id="the-pieces-that-matter-most">The pieces that matter most</h2><p><strong>Security monitoring &amp; SIEM.</strong> A SIEM aggregates logs, correlates them, and raises alerts. The<br>exam — and the job — care less about which product and more about <em>what a good alert looks like</em><br>and why correlation beats single-source logging. Time synchronisation (NTP) matters more than it<br>sounds: without it, you cannot reconstruct an incident timeline across hosts.</p><p><strong>Alert triage.</strong> The core SOC skill. Know the difference cold: a <strong>false positive</strong> wastes<br>analyst time; a <strong>false negative</strong> is the attack you missed — and the asymmetry is why detuning<br>noisy rules is dangerous. "Impossible travel" (a login from London then Tokyo eleven minutes<br>later) is the canonical triage scenario.</p><p><strong>Incident response.</strong> Memorise the NIST SP 800-61 lifecycle in order — Preparation → Detection &amp;<br>Analysis → Containment, Eradication &amp; Recovery → Post-incident activity — because the exam loves to<br>scramble it. The real-world lesson hidden in the order: you cannot respond well to an incident you<br>did not prepare for.</p><p><strong>Email security.</strong> SPF authorises sending hosts, DKIM signs the message, DMARC sets policy using<br>both — in that dependency order. This is the most-tested trap in the domain and the control that<br>stops the phishing that starts most breaches.</p><h2 id="the-portfolio-connection">The portfolio connection</h2><p>The defensive discipline here is exactly what a Wazuh SIEM home lab exercises: write a detection<br>rule, generate the attack, confirm the alert fires, measure how long it took. CTF writeups that<br>include a <em>defender perspective</em> — the Event IDs and Sigma rules an attack would trip — are the<br>same offence-to-detection bridge this domain rewards.</p><h2 id="exam-traps-to-watch">Exam traps to watch</h2><ul><li>RPO vs RTO inverted (RPO = tolerable data loss; RTO = tolerable downtime).</li><li>SPF/DKIM/DMARC roles swapped.</li><li>Treating a false negative as "less bad" than a false positive — it is the opposite.</li></ul><p><em>Full domain notes, scenario drills, and a quick-reference card are in the</em><br><em><a href="https://github.com/rootdrifter/sec-plus-notes">sec-plus-notes</a> repo.</em></p>
