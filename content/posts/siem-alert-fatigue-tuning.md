<blockquote>Practitioner notes. Grounded in SY0-701 Domain 4 study material and an in-progress Wazuh home SIEM lab. No vendor affiliation; no production telemetry reproduced here.</blockquote><p>Ask a SOC how good its detection is and you will usually get a rule count. Ask the analysts on shift and you will get a different number: how many alerts they closed today without reading them properly. Those two numbers are connected, and the second one is the one that decides whether a real intrusion gets caught. Most SOCs do not have a detection problem — they have a signal-to-noise problem, and tuning is the only thing that fixes it.</p><h2 id="the-arithmetic-that-creates-alert-fatigue">The arithmetic that creates alert fatigue</h2><p>The core issue is base rates. Suppose a rule is 99% accurate and fires on an event class where genuine malice is 1 in 10,000 occurrences. Run 100,000 events through it and you get roughly 10 true alerts buried in about 1,000 false ones. The rule is "99% accurate" and 99% of what it produces is noise. No analyst triages that honestly for long — they habituate, and habituation is precisely the failure mode: the classification you care about is not the false positive, it is the <strong>false negative an exhausted analyst creates by skimming</strong>.</p><p>The four outcomes are worth keeping in front of you, because the dangerous quadrant is the quiet one:</p><table><thead><tr><th>Outcome</th><th>Alert?</th><th>Real event?</th><th>Cost</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>True positive</td><td>Yes</td><td>Yes</td><td>The job working as intended</td></tr><tr><td>False positive</td><td>Yes</td><td>No</td><td>Analyst time, habituation</td></tr><tr><td>True negative</td><td>No</td><td>No</td><td>None — steady state</td></tr><tr><td>False negative</td><td>No</td><td>Yes</td><td><strong>A missed attack</strong> — and FP load manufactures these</td></tr></tbody></table><p>That last cell is the whole argument. Untuned rules do not just waste time; they convert true positives into effective false negatives by burying them.</p><h2 id="why-more-rules-make-it-worse">Why "more rules" makes it worse</h2><p>Rule count is a vanity metric. Every rule you enable ships with a false-positive rate, and those rates are additive across the queue while analyst attention is fixed. Importing a 500-rule community pack into a SIEM that was coping yesterday is how you get a queue that nobody trusts tomorrow. The uncomfortable discipline is the opposite one: a detection that nobody has reviewed, that converts to an incident 0% of the time, and that fires forty times a day is not coverage — it is camouflage for the alert that matters. Retire it or fix it.</p><h2 id="the-tuning-loop">The tuning loop</h2><p>Tuning is not a one-off cleanup; it is the operating rhythm of a functioning SOC. The loop is short:</p><ol><li><strong>Validate</strong> — confirm what the alert actually reflects before acting. Acting on an unvalidated alert wastes effort and can cause a self-inflicted outage (blocking a VIP's account on a travel-login anomaly is the classic).</li><li><strong>Classify</strong> — TP, FP, or benign-true-positive (the event happened, and it is authorised: a pentest, a backup job, an admin doing admin things).</li><li><strong>Adjust</strong> — and here order matters. Prefer, in sequence: add context (asset criticality, user role, known-maintenance windows) → narrow scope (this rule should never have applied to the build servers) → raise thresholds (5 failures, not 3) → suppress as a last resort, with an expiry date.</li><li><strong>Measure</strong> — did the change move the per-rule numbers, and did detection still fire in the next purple-team or simulated run?</li></ol><p>The distinction in step 3 is the one that separates tuning from decay. <strong>Suppression hides output; fixing logic improves it.</strong> A suppression without an expiry is a detection gap with a clear conscience.</p><h2 id="what-to-measure-per-rule-not-per-soc">What to measure — per rule, not per SOC</h2><p>SOC-wide MTTD is a useful executive number and a useless engineering one. The numbers that drive tuning are per-detection:</p><ul><li><strong>False-positive rate per rule</strong> — the direct measure of noise contribution.</li><li><strong>Alert-to-incident conversion</strong> — of everything this rule fired, how much became a real investigation? Rules near 0% need work or retirement.</li><li><strong>Time-to-triage</strong> — if analysts consistently park a rule's alerts, they have already told you what they think of it.</li><li><strong>Detection latency</strong> per technique — how long from event to alert, measured by replaying the attack, not by assumption.</li></ul><p>That last one only exists if you test detections deliberately: run the technique in a controlled way, confirm the rule fires, record how long it took. This is exactly what I am building a home Wazuh lab around — scenario-by-scenario detection validation with per-scenario time-to-detect tracking — because a rule that has never been exercised against its own technique is a hypothesis, not a detection.</p><h2 id="where-soar-and-ueba-actually-fit">Where SOAR and UEBA actually fit</h2><p>Two technologies get sold as the cure for fatigue, and both help only after tuning, not instead of it. <strong>SOAR</strong> automates the repetitive halves of triage — enrichment, reputation lookups, ticket creation, containment playbooks for the high-volume, low-ambiguity alert classes like commodity phishing. Automating an untuned queue just generates noise faster and occasionally automates the self-inflicted outage. <strong>UEBA</strong> attacks the problem from the other side: instead of static thresholds it baselines per-user and per-entity behaviour, which catches the "valid credentials, wrong behaviour" class that fixed rules are structurally bad at. Its trade-off is the training period and a new flavour of false positive (people change roles, travel, and work odd hours).</p><h2 id="the-takeaway">The takeaway</h2><p>Detection engineering is a feedback discipline. The rule you write is the start of its life, not the end: it earns its place in the queue by converting to incidents, it gets context and scope adjustments as the environment changes, and it gets retired when it stops paying rent. A SOC that tunes relentlessly with 80 rules will catch more than one that hoards 800 — because the constraint was never rule coverage. It was analyst attention, and tuning is how you spend that attention on the alerts that are real.</p>
